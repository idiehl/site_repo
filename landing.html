Atlas: Apocalypse

To Weather the Storm

Dedcation and Intent

This work is the product of a lifetime. None of the sections here have been the product of individual investigation. Without the countless others who have contributed to the various fields visited on this site, any rigorous inquiry into truth would have fallen flat. Whether the contents of the site resemble reality in any useful way remains to be seen. Someone else stated it simply: "If I have seen far, it is because I have stood upon the shoulders of giants who came before me.".

Producing the site has come at a great cost. Without the support, compassion, and continued understanding of others, neither time the nor energy to construct the site could have been afforded. In conducting this investigation, an insurmountable debt has accumulated both financially and personally. To realize a functional website has been dirty work. It has however, not been erected in error. Like Jacob's Ladder to heaven, this foundation has been laid in full sight of gods and men. Let these feeble columns stand as a monument to hope - both personal and collective - for a future free of doubt, regret, and fear.

Some say life itself is a gift that comes at a price. Blood spilled is blood owed. It's true that many cynics regard it as a curse. Without question, a compelling argument can be made against the quality of our time spent here. Each day we face insurmountable odds. We endure an apparently meaningless existence void of an overt purpose, direction, or structure. All our castles will in time crumble to sand. No doubt, this fact is bitterly regrettable - that we face the rising tide each day. All we love will wash away. It's enough to drive men mad. God is cruel and vengeful in his retribution against us. Thus, we stand at an apparent crossroads in our every step. Here we are confronted by an impossible dilemma. We've each inherited some measure of intelligence and awareness of our surroundings. Some say the soul is compelled to the service of goodness and compassion in understanding. If we accept that in being we embody an inherent inclination toward goodness, should we strive to build up the world around us in an effort to impose order on our surroundings? To work in earnest toward a relief from human limitation can only begin in earnest from the recognition of our own ignorance and folly. As we move through life, we leave a trail of blood in our wake. As we encounter the world in all our righteous intention, we are drawn in every direction toward injustice. Should we be drawn outward toward opportunity we move into a carnival of horrors and violence. Men have, without fail, become lost in this wilderness in their endeavors.

Let the first truth presented here as wisdom in offering be this: If you trust in hope and set out in search of disorder, that you might set it right, turn first inward toward you own heart. Turn inwards and look back. If we should be so confident in our character as to impose it upon the world around us, we must know ourselves fully in all our potential and folly. We set out as wanderers in every direction. All around us we begin building up walls. Claiming ownership and possession of everything under our dominion. We make alliances, form bonds, and take vows. Insodoing we allow others to enter under our protection and authority. Men may in time take wives. Bare children. All this and more we do in certainty of our goodness. If a person should have any sense for reason, in establishing their domain they simultaneously enter into conflict and disagreement. Being self-assured, we passionately defend ourselves against hostility. Against our enemies. Against our peers. Against our friends. Against our loved ones. Many turn to cruelty and vengeance toward others. Without much effort, we find ourselves at war with everything around us in our quest for knowledge. They say war is hell. Being in a state of hostility inspires hatred toward our enemies. Some become murderous toward others. We may in desperation abandon the possibility for reconciliation - the bonds between us are broken in anger. Men become monsters.

How much disagreement can be explained simply as a difference in perspective? The first problem of being is our individuality - our finitude. In all our wisdom, we still lack awareness. We shed light on everything around us and all we can conceive of. However, the very fact of our personal separation from others necessitates our fallibility and ignorance. Each of us stands as an island. Forever cut off from the truth of those around us. Though we may agree on much, even our nearest friends stand apart from us. Nothing may bridge this gap. In trust and friendship we may grow in together partnership and closeness, sharing many qualities. Those who stand apart from us, or against us however, grow apart in their experience. Without much effort, we often find that people grow apart in time. While our domain may yet stand in apparent harmony with justice, enemies gather all around us. In opposition with others we share little. We know of our personal goodness and the quality of our experience, but we regard others with deep distrust and suspicion under duress. When confronted with an apparent contradiction between our personal observation and the experience of others, we cling tightly to our own conviction. In doubt, we trust our eyes and our experience first. We'd sooner face the possibility that humans possess the capacity for evil than consider our own lack of knowledge. In fear, people tend to trust the fear they know.

Everyone has a unique response to conflict. Under duress, many would willingly submit themselves to subjugation or subordination in favor of continued hostility with peers or loved ones. Others are motivated by conflict and competition, so long as some semblance of structure underlies conflict. However, in many cases people spend a substantial amount of their lives engaged in quiet aggression against those around them - even those they claim to love. For most of us, we find peace in the quiet of solitude and isolation. We may spend the majority of our days in opposition with others - suspended in a kind of tense stalemate with those around us. We negotiate our position through compromise. Interaction with some may call upon us to resort to threat, intimidation, manipulation, and dishonesty. At the surface we manage to occupy the same space without incident, but a silent war wages amongst us.

We often forget that all our struggle began with the basic assumption of our own integrity. Some may spend a lifetime without turning away from the unending calamity that surrounds us in our daily lives. Managing our daily affairs is intoxicating and hypnotic. Our every waking moment is spent fixated on the drama as it unfolds. We lie awake at night, bewildered at the terrifying possibilities all around us and the threat of what may happen next. The faith we place in our own capacity to navigate our affairs is unflinching - we are utterly assured of our ability to respond effectively to all life's challenges.

To genuinely reflect on our behavior, or to consider fault in any substantial way for some, rarely occurs. We may periodically submit ourselves to blame, guilt, punishment, and embrassment when pressured to do so, or when we are certain of the outcome. But we are reluctant to admit the possibility that we have been flawed in our core beliefs, or that we are incapable of recognizing truth from falsity. Even moreso are we opposed to confessing when our commitment to integrity is put under question. It's rare to meet a person who openly admits to being a bad person - to being cruel, indifferent, selfish, or vindictive. Men desperately cling to the image of themselves as virtuous when confronted. Even in error, our underlying intentions must never be placed under serious scrutiny - for many, this alone may provoke them to violence. The most important thing society offers an individual is the opportunity to defend himself through communication under the protection of the state.

We're fortunate to live in a state where we manage to find relative success against the odds. Culture has enabled billions of people to live together and work toward our individual pursuits. It's possible to sustain the belief in our own personal goodness and to surround ourselves with others who support this view. As long as we are willing to maintain a relative similarity to those around us, to avoid friction and opposition, the illusion of belonging to a community of people who share in the commitment to good is entirely sustainable. If we follow the rules of society and play well with others, our community defends our quality and the value of our experience. Moreover, the state has managed to execute justice against criminality and deviance. If we are to trust appearances, then together we are realizing a better world. In contributing to our society, we work toward a future free from conflict - we believe wholly that our effort is not in vain and that we have realized victory and peace through our commitment.

Many are shocked when they experience tragedy for the first time. While intellectually we have understood the possibility or inevitability of suffering and death, we seem to have the ability to limit its influence on our emotions. In hope, we trust that through offering our most passionate loyalty and labor in the service of good, it may not be in vain. Many adopt the view that beyond appearance, the universe operates according to a perfect order. The hand of God defends the truly devoted from the worst fate has to offer. The force exercising final authority over fate, we suppose, must resemble us in our kindness and love. We may realize freedom from fear altogether in the service of truth. All that's demanded of us is our faith, worship, and obedience. If we manage to embody these qualities in life through learning subjugation and service to God, all our faults will be forgiven. In exchange for our service, we secure our position on the afterlife. The kingdom of heaven has already been realized. To gain entry we need only submit to authority in life - God asserts that we may rest easily. We need not spend life in toil, or concern ourselves about building a more positive future for future generations. Life is but a dream. If we can only trust in hope, all the terror and agony we feel toward death, pain, and judgement may be washed away and our lives may be spent in relief.

The church and other religious institutions have recognized for themselves the importance of forgiveness. What they offer humanity is of immense value - few have considered the hidden cost associated with the religious offering. Organized religion effectively acts as the industrialization of virtue. As an organization they manufacture virtue in the form of good deeds and charity. Every member may take credit for the works of the church - they may take these offerings as evidence of their personal penance and reconciliation. Individuals in the organization need only speak the vows, and observe its tenants to gain membership. Many who fall upon hard times willingly utter the words in times of desperation. Their directness is in itself a profound indication of their potent influence. We need only confess our sins before God and man - to do so in truth and to ask forgiveness.

Such a simple thing. We need not posses wisdom or virtue. Our salvation is granted by the grace of god - and through grace alone. The great religions of this world have recognized the truth of our condition. We are irrevocably and incurably flawed. Each of us is drowning in a sea of illusion. It poisons us and all we touch. Men are sickened by it. No matter our position, all our efforts turn to folly. The church provides men a way forward. To admit one's foolishness and wickedness - to call out to the highest authority. To look out in every direction and offer acknowledgement of one's error.

So far one truth has been here presented: To look first to oneself. The act of confession resembles the insight in many ways and may possess similar value. Many of us have encountered religious advocates throughout our lives who claim to have been redeemed and empowered through God's grace. Some who align with the church are undoubtedly of great character, but through experience we often come to realize an underlying hypocrisy in evangelists. While they have themselves been gifted God's grace, they withhold it from others in their interactions. Their most profaned offenses have been accounted for and forgiven, but many are quick to judgement and condemnation. Forgiveness is a gift offered up to the few who can afford it. The shortcoming of organized religion is that they reveal to us the greatest insight - our personal fault, and in response they provide us with a means of forgiveness in certainty. Thereby, men may indulge in sin without concern. Having committed egregious acts against another, they presume to seek relief from guilt and shame without making an appeal to the victim of their crimes. Their crimes have been paid for in full through the blood of Christ. Men become gluttonous and bloated in their indulgence through the church. The most horrific offenses have been acted out by men of the church - and paid through Christ's sacrifice.

In times long forgotten to history, men weren't afforded the luxury provided by the church. Before the establishment of statehood or kings on Earth, the world was a vast wilderness. No safety or security had yet been realized through just governance. No law governed the affairs of men in their transactions. The rule of the wild was the only dominion on Earth. Crime committed was crime paid - often in blood. Doubtless, by today's standards many crimes were committed unchecked. That is, only power defined authority and justice. Life itself was the most valuable thing a person possessed. None had been afforded a line of credit. Men could not spend what they did not possess. From the jungle, civilization emerged along with currency.

The earliest religions realized the power of sacrificial offering as compensation. The brutal wilderness separating men could be tamed through offerings of peace. In time, some began accumulating wealth and abundance. Some learned that it was more profitable to spare the life of another when they had something of value to offer. Governance and kingship came into being quite naturally as wealthy men encountered those with little. Law emerged as kings realized that men would willingly submit to servitude when faced with the decision of life or death. The state of total war between men gradually transformed as all the world was brought to heel in submission to authority. A new hierarchy appeared among the population - a distinct line separating those who decree law and possess wealth from those who submit to slavery and indebtedness.

We tend to hold contempt toward the system of hierarchy dividing us. While it's true, historically it has been the cause of enormous conflict, we often fail to consider its value. We're each born into the world as outsiders - in every way alien to the state. A threat to the system in place. The state offers us terms for peace - terms for our unconditional surrender: Obey the law or pay the cost. Refuse these terms and pay in blood. Under the protection of the state we wield great power. So long as we observe the laws in place, no crime against us goes unpunished. Words become more powerful than bullets - no harm may come to us, no matter our position. We're free to accumulate wealth and exercise dominion over others within the confines of the law.

At present, those born into this world have entered the stage at a late hour. Many have come before us. We're born into hostile territory spanning this whole Earth. Nothing has been left unclaimed - we have no alternative but to confront the powers that have here been established. As autonomous individual we inherit a set of inalienable rights. The first of those is to meet the state with hostility and open rebellion. We may accept the terms that have been presented - our unconditional surrender in exchange for servitude. Or we may seek to enter into negotiation with the powers that be in an effort to assert our individuality and sovereignty. To set ourselves apart from the state, from God, and from any other individual or collective seeking to enter into union. In each case we have been offered a truce. Without question every other party we have encountered has met us with hostility - the nature of our conflict disguises our wounds from us, but each of us bares the scars. Against God we have endured the truth of death and fate - we suffer the nature of our human embodiment. Of our limitation, fallibility, and vulnerability against men and in life. Our universe is apparently isolated from the kingdom of heaven and falls outside its jurisdiction. The state has surrounded us on all sides, claiming exclusive possession over the resources required to sustain life. It has subjected us to impoverishment and scarcity. It has violated our personal sovereignty and denied the right to privacy. It imposes the rule of law and the threat of imprisonment. It has forced us into indoctrination and teaches us a means of communication so that we may respond to propaganda. We must subscribe to the picture of the world and of our surroundings that has been presented to us. A picture of war waiting just outside our door. Finally we have endured humiliation as we have been forced to communicate with our peers and participate in public education in such a way as to convincingly portray our complete ideological indoctrination.

In opposition to God and the state, we have each encountered the "other" first and foremost. First, we encounter the Mother - her love and willingness to sacrifice goes without question as we each emerged from her womb. She nourishes us from her own bodily stores to sustain life. Her authority is complete and goes without question. While in time through neglect or abuse, our love may turn to bitterness as we grow into our individuality, any pain she may cause through opposing our needs and wants is simply accepted without want of repayment. The bond between mother and child presupposes an inherent trust and forgiveness.

The bond between Father and child for some has a similar dynamic. Just as the infant surrenders into nourishment and intimacy he has toward the Mother, the Father finds in the son an opportunity to establish his own kingdom. Before the state, or any notion of God enters the mind of a child, the Father embodies a first confrontation with authority. Just as an individual is powerless alone against the state or God's hand, a child is helpless against the Father. No crime against him goes unpunished. No personal insight may exceed his wisdom. If the Father is good, as the child grows he is allowed to negotiate his position and develop independence. To refuse this and instead rely on force, threat, and intimidation against the child to retain one's position constitutes abuse. The good Father willingly shares in his wisdom and support until the child no longer relies on it or wants it.

At every level, the individual must decide their response to the power dynamic occurring between themselves and the other. Whether to defend their personal sovereignty or to become a dependent. Our identity becomes a reflection of the nature of our relationships. Humans are complex - we develop a distinct vocabulary in order to mediate each of our commitments. All our effort goes into avoiding a deficit. To avoid debt and to work toward abundance and luxury.
